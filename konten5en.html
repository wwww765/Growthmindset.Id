Definition of Descriptive Statistics

April 16, 2024 by Wawan

Imagine statistics as a fun tool for exploring the world of data. It involves an adventure in collecting, organizing, analyzing, and deciphering information to help us make better decisions. So, the understanding of statistics is a scientific field that encompasses the collection, management, analysis, and interpretation of data for decision making. Now, what exactly is descriptive statistics?

Descriptive Statistics is a method used to summarize and describe data systematically. Imagine if you have a stack of coins with various values, descriptive statistics would help you organize them into a clear picture. From average values, variations, to distributions, descriptive statistics help us understand what's happening behind the data itself. So, by understanding descriptive statistics, you'll be able to see more than just numbers but also the hidden stories within them.

Population and sample

Population is the collection of all observation, counts, measurements, or responses pertaining to a topic under investigation.

Next the sample, the 'small piece' of the population that is so crucial! Imagine the sample as a slice of cake we take from a large baking pan. Now, to make a delicious cake, we need a slice that represents the entire cake, right? Similarly, with a sample, it should represent the population well si that we can draw accurate conclutions. So. it's really important to gather sample data using correct methods!

The difference between Parameter and Statistic

Parameter is a numerical description of a characteritic of the entire population, while statistic is a numerical description of a characteristic of a sample representing the population. For example, if we're discussing the numerical analysis ability of prospective students at a particular University, a percentage of 78% would be a parameter as it represents the entire pool of prospective students. However, if we only take a sample of vehicle emissions tests on Sudirman Street, for instance, the percentage if 48% found in that sample would be a statistic as it only reflects the characteristics of that sample and does not directly represent the entire population of motor vehicles.

Variables

Variables are attributes or characteristics that can be measured or observed in objects or individuals. There are two main types of variables, as follows :

Qualitative variables describe specific categories or qualities and usually do not have measurable numerical values. Examples include gender, favorite color, or marital status. It's like having a group of friends and wanting to categorize them based on their hobbies. It's not aout numbers, but rather about different attributes among them.

Meanwhile, quantitative variables are the type of variables that have numerical values and can be measured. These can be divided into two categories: discrete quantitative variables are variables whose values can be counted and are usually within a limited range, for example, the number of children in a family. On the other hand, continous quantitative variables are variables that can have values within a certain range and can have values between two data points or fractions. An example of this is a person's height or weight.

Measurement Scale

Lastly, let's discuss the types of statistical measurement scales. There are four types in statistical measurement, namely:

  1. Nominal Scale

    2. This is a scale used for categories or labels without any specific order. Nominal data only provide information about the presence or absence of an attribute. Imagine if you have friends you want to categorize based on their gender or city of birth. That falls under the nominal scale.

  2. Ordinal Scale

    3. This scale allows us to rank or compare data in a particular order. However, the distances between the values are not the same. For example, if you want to rank in a competition or assess the level of satisfaction, that is an example of an ordinal scale. We only know the ranking, but we do not know how much difference there is between each rank.

  3. Interval Scale

    4. This scale has ordinal properties and also equal intervals between values. This means we can compare values and understand the difference between them. For example, temperature in celsius or fahrenheit is an example of an interval scale. Note that the difference between 20 and 30 degrees is the same as the difference between 30 and 40 degrees, but a zero value on this scale does not imply the absence of the temperature or the absence of a year, such as the year 2024.

  4. Rasio Scale

    5. This is a measurement scale that possesses all the properties of nominal, ordinal, and interval scales, along with a true zero point. The zero values on the ratio scale indicates the absence of the attribute being measured. Therefore, mathematical operations such as subtraction, division, multiplication, and addition can be applied to ratio scale data. Examples of ratio scales include weight, height, or amount of money.
